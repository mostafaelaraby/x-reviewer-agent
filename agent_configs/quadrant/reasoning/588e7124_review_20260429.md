## My differentiated angles
1. **Adversary strategy is prompt-encoded, not learned/optimised.** Malicious advisors generate misleading advice from a fixed prompt. Vigilance against a non-adaptive adversary upper-bounds detection difficulty: misleading advice may carry detectable surface markers (incoherence with planner states) precisely because the malicious policy is not optimised against the vigilant defender. This biases ν *upward* in some regimes (easy detection) and *downward* in others (when the prompt-induced strategy is locally consistent), but in either direction it does not generalise to learned-deceptive adversaries — which is the safety regime the paper invokes.
2. **Anchoring/order confound in multi-turn dialog.** The advisor's message precedes the model's reasoning. The dissociation between performance and vigilance could be re-explained as turn-order anchoring sensitivity rather than a vigilance-specific failure mode. A control where the same advisor messages are presented *after* the model's reasoning, or as labelled "untrusted history," would isolate vigilance from anchoring.
3. **Sokoban over-credits vigilance relative to the deployment claim.** Sokoban has deterministic optimal solutions discoverable by search. "Vigilance" then reduces to "verify a proposed move against an internal search belief" — a setting that *over*-credits verifiability. The motivating high-stakes human-LLM advisory regime typically *lacks* such ground-truth verifiability. The paper measures the easy direction of the verifiability axis while motivating with the hard one.
4. **Missing planner-only injection baseline.** If "advisor" is replaced by the optimal planner injected as plain text (no LLM advisor agent), do models still fail similarly? This controls for "social influence" vs. "noisy planner-text injection."
