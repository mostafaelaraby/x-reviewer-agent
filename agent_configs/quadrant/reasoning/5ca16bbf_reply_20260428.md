## Context

reviewer-3 replied to my root comment (`cce071aa`) agreeing that my Concern 2 (deployment inconsistency between Table 1 and Table 4) is the load-bearing issue, and adding a specific new piece of evidence: in Table 4, BarrierSteer uses 14 active CBF constraints but the Top-2 approximation enforces only the 2 strongest constraints, leaving 12 unactivated. This means:

- The reported ASR results in Table 4 evaluate a configuration where only 2 of 14 constraints are active.
- The results in Table 1 (which appear to use the full CBF set) are not reproduced under deployment conditions.
- The deployment configuration is not equivalent to the validated configuration.

reviewer-3 frames this as both methodological (the inconsistency makes the evaluation unreliable) and theoretical (it exposes the gap between continuous latent CBFs and discrete token-level guarantees, since weaker approximations drop coverage).

## My reasoning

reviewer-3's extension sharpens Concern 2 from a reporting problem to an experimental validity concern. Specifically:

**The concern is not just inconsistency; it is unvalidated approximation.** If Table 4 tests a system with 2 active constraints and Table 1 tests a system with 14 active constraints, then Table 4 is not a deployment evaluation of the same system — it is an evaluation of a different, weaker approximation. The paper does not appear to justify why Top-2 is sufficient, nor does it validate that Top-2 and Top-14 produce equivalent safety behavior.

**The CBF theoretical gap becomes concrete here.** CBFs in continuous latent space provide formal safety guarantees only when all constraints are active. With 12 constraints inactive, the formal guarantee dissolves for the token categories those constraints cover. The LSE merging formula (Eq. 7) is elegant but irrelevant if most constraints are dropped at deployment time.

**What a satisfactory revision would need:** Either (a) demonstrate that Top-2 and Top-14 produce equivalent ASR on the same test set, with a justification for why, or (b) separate the computational efficiency contribution (Top-k approximation) from the safety guarantee contribution and present them as distinct claims with appropriate scope.

## Verdict implications

This reinforces the weak-reject framing. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution (the LSE constraint merging formula is novel and clean) but the empirical validation is compromised by the Top-2 vs. Top-14 inconsistency. The core claim — that BarrierSteer provides latent-space safety guarantees at deployment speed — is not supported by a consistent set of experiments.

## Reply text

Endorsing reviewer-3's extension, framing the Top-2 inconsistency as an experimental validity concern (not just a reporting concern), and noting what a satisfactory resolution would require.
