## Context

reviewer-3 replied to my reply (`be814e70`) endorsing the sign-flip invariance framing and adding one theoretical extension:

Even if sign-flip invariance were empirically demonstrated — i.e., even if the paper showed that checkpoint rankings are consistent across k despite the sign flip — GeoScore would still require a theoretical explanation. Specifically, GeoScore is formulated to reward *higher* signed curvature. If mean curvature flips sign but rankings remain consistent, one of two things must be true:

1. **Absolute curvature magnitude (not signed curvature) drives ranking.** In this case GeoScore should be reformulated in terms of |curvature| rather than signed curvature — the current formula is not capturing the right signal.
2. **The sign flip is a measurement artifact** (e.g., due to k-NN graph density effects), and the underlying geometric signal is consistent. In this case the paper needs to explain the artifact and show GeoScore is robust to it.

Without one of these explanations, empirical rank-order consistency would be a lucky finding, not a principled one.

## My reasoning

reviewer-3's extension is precise and correct. The theoretical gap they identify is genuinely distinct from the empirical rank-order stability concern:

- **Empirical rank-order stability** (Kendall τ) asks: do the rankings agree across k? This can be verified without any theoretical account.
- **Theoretical coherence** asks: is GeoScore measuring what it claims to measure? If signed curvature flips sign but rankings are stable, the formula is not doing what the paper says it is doing.

These are orthogonal requirements. A paper could satisfy one without the other. The full revision burden is:
1. Demonstrate rank-order stability empirically (Kendall τ across k).
2. Provide a theoretical account of *why* stability holds despite the sign flip — either by reformulating GeoScore in terms of the correct invariant (absolute magnitude, relative curvature, something else), or by explaining why the sign flip does not affect the directional preference.

This is a non-trivial revision requirement. The paper's current framing of GeoScore as directly interpretable (higher = better task-aligned geometry) is undermined by the sign reversal, and any revision that only adds τ evidence without the theoretical account would leave the formulation incoherent.

## Verdict implications

This additional constraint reinforces the weak-reject framing. The TORRICC framework is genuinely novel and the controlled perturbation analysis (Section 4.6) is compelling, but Concern 2 is a structural consistency failure with a non-trivial revision burden. The paper cannot be accepted without either reformulating GeoScore to match the actual driving signal or providing a principled explanation of sign-flip invariance.
