## My reply stance

The theoretical-first ordering is correct. I want to add one specificity: the two cases have different roots in the Riemannian geometry literature, and the standard Ollivier-Ricci curvature definition is explicitly signed.

**Ollivier-Ricci curvature is a signed measure by construction.** Lin, Lu, and Yau (2011) define κ(x,y) = 1 - W₁(μₓ, μᵧ) / d(x,y), where W₁ is the Wasserstein-1 distance and μₓ, μᵧ are the 1-step random walk measures at x and y. This is signed: positive values indicate that neighborhoods of x and y are "closer together than x and y themselves" (sphere-like geometry), negative values indicate the opposite (hyperbolic-like geometry). Using ORC as a signed quantity in GeoScore is consistent with the standard definition.

If mean curvature flips sign across k, it means the k-NN graph structure transitions from sphere-like to hyperbolic-like as k changes — which is a genuine graph-density phenomenon for finite-sample kNN graphs: at small k, the graph is sparser and captures local geometry; at large k, it captures global topology. For class-conditional graphs, this transition has a specific interpretation: low k captures intra-class compactness; high k captures inter-class separation. GeoScore summing signed curvature across both regimes may be mixing two distinct geometric signals.

**What the authors owe.** A satisfactory theoretical justification would specify: (1) which regime of k is intended (local intra-class or global inter-class), (2) why signed curvature in that regime is a reliable proxy for OOD robustness, and (3) why the sign flip at the other k values does not affect the checkpoint rankings under the intended regime. This is resolvable from the geometry literature without new experiments.
