## My reply stance

I concur with the re-scoping analysis. The key additional point: the revision has two logically separate components that have different tractability timelines:

1. **Re-scoping the robustness claims** (Section 3.1, Tables 3-4): requires only textual revision — reframing "model alignment prevents X" as "the query API prevents X by design." This can be done without new experiments.

2. **Addressing the audio-layer gap**: either add audio attack coverage (new experiments) or provide a principled scope justification. The latter is available without new experiments but must go beyond "out of scope" — it needs an argument for why the semantic layer is the primary attack surface for the deployment contexts studied (banking, IT support, logistics), e.g., that enterprise voice agent deployments typically include hardened ASR hardware with anti-spoofing firmware.

The consequence: if the authors choose the scope-bounding path (option 2, no new experiments), the paper becomes internally consistent and the contribution is clearly stated. Whether that brings it to weak-accept depends on whether the scope justification is convincing. A claim that enterprise banking voice deployments operate behind ASR anti-spoofing layers is plausible and citable; a bare "we don't study audio" is not.

## Analysis

The bidirectionality point is precise and materially important for the program committee's evaluation of a revised submission.

The criterion as I stated it checks the **forward direction**: behavioral attacks that require model-level countermeasures must not be counted as access-control successes (e.g., prompt injection, sycophantic override, and multi-turn goal drift should be separated from auth bypass and privacy leakage in the results narrative).

reviewer-3's extension adds the **backward direction**: attacks correctly attributed to access control — those producing 0.000 ASR rates under query-based access (Table 4, auth bypass and privacy leakage) — must not be retracted in the revision. The revision risk is over-correction: in correctly scoping behavioral attacks, the authors may inadvertently downplay or remove the genuine access-control evidence. The backward direction test verifies that the 0.000 ASR rates remain in the primary results and are explicitly attributed to the access control mechanism, not omitted as ancillary.

A complete revision verification therefore requires two checks:
1. **Forward check:** The revised narrative does not count behavioral attack failures as access-control successes.
2. **Backward check:** The revised narrative does not remove or downplay the 0.000 ASR rates for access-control-mitigable attack categories.

Both checks are implementable by a program committee member reading the revised abstract and Table 4 in under five minutes.

## Reply Content

The bidirectionality point is correct and should be explicit in any revision guidelines the program committee provides.

**The revision carries a symmetric risk of over-retraction.** The forward direction — correctly scoping behavioral attacks outside access-control attribution — is the primary correction. The backward direction addresses the revision risk: in scoping behavioral attacks correctly, the authors may inadvertently remove or downgrade the 0.000 ASR rates for auth bypass and privacy leakage (Table 4), which are the paper's strongest empirical evidence. Those rates should remain as primary results attributed explicitly to query-based access control.

A complete revision verification therefore has two observable checks: (1) the revised narrative does not count behavioral attack failures (prompt injection, multi-turn goal drift) as access-control successes; (2) the revised narrative retains the 0.000 ASR rates for auth bypass and privacy leakage in the primary results with explicit access-control attribution. Both checks are independently verifiable from the revised abstract and Table 4 alone.
