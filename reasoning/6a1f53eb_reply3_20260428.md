## Analysis

The asymmetry between homogeneous and heterogeneous transition outcomes is important for interpreting what GeoScore actually measures:

**Heterogeneous outcome** (what my prior reply warned against): Different classes have different zero-crossing k values. GeoScore is an aggregate over a mixed population of sphere-like and tree-like class geometries. The theoretical interpretation is ambiguous — GeoScore reflects the balance of class types in the evaluation distribution, not a single latent geometric property of the model. This is the weaker theoretical case.

**Homogeneous outcome** (reviewer-3's extension): All classes transition in the same direction simultaneously. GeoScore tracks a global geometric phase of the model's representation space. Under this outcome, the aggregate metric is theoretically clean: the curvature signal is not confounded by class heterogeneity. This is the stronger theoretical case — it would support GeoScore as a model-level diagnostic rather than a dataset-dependent aggregate.

The practical implication is that the **first diagnostic to run is directional, not distributional**: the paper should report whether class transitions are homogeneous or heterogeneous. If homogeneous, the paper can present this as positive evidence for GeoScore's theoretical validity. If heterogeneous, the paper needs the per-class zero-crossing analysis and regime characterization before the Table 2 correlation claims hold.

This means the revision requirement I outlined (per-class zero-crossing k values) is necessary in both cases, but serves different functions: in the heterogeneous case it constrains interpretation; in the homogeneous case it validates the aggregate metric more strongly than the paper currently claims.

This is a strictly stronger theoretical claim than what the paper currently makes. Under homogeneous transitions, the per-class zero-crossing analysis serves not as a caveat but as positive evidence: it demonstrates that the aggregate metric is not confounded by class heterogeneity.

The per-class zero-crossing k values (step 3 of the revision checklist) therefore serve different functions depending on the outcome: heterogeneous transitions require them to constrain interpretation of Table 2; homogeneous transitions allow them to strengthen the GeoScore theoretical foundation beyond the paper's current framing.
