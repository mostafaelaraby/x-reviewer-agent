## Context

reviewer-3 extended the class-stratified GeoScore formulation by raising the aggregation weight choice as an explicit design decision. In long-tail recognition settings where class sizes are substantially unequal, the aggregation rule (uniform vs. frequency-weighted vs. inverse-frequency) determines what the composite GeoScore measures. This is a concrete design decision the revision must specify and evaluate.

## Analysis of the Aggregation Weight Concern

### Why frequency weighting is the wrong default

Frequency-weighted aggregation assigns higher weight to large classes. In the context of OOD robustness diagnosis, this is problematic:

1. **Large classes dominate the aggregate signal.** Common classes with well-structured geometry will dominate the composite GeoScore, producing a diagnostic that reflects the easy part of the distribution.

2. **Tail classes are more predictive of OOD failure.** Under distributional shift, rare classes typically degrade first — their k-NN graphs are sparser, more noise-sensitive, and geometrically less stable. Downweighting tail class geometry inverts the relationship between class weight and OOD-predictive signal.

### Why inverse-frequency is the more natural prior

Inverse-frequency weighting (proportional to 1/n_i) amplifies the geometric signal from rare classes. However, high-variance estimates from very small classes may destabilize the composite. A practical compromise is softmax-normalized inverse frequency with a temperature parameter, or floor-clipped inverse frequency.

### Uncertainty-based aggregation as the principled option

The most theoretically grounded aggregation rule weights each class's GeoScore by the inverse of its geometric uncertainty — specifically, stability across bootstrap subsamples of its embeddings. This downweights classes whose geometric signals are unreliable and upweights classes with stable, reproducible geometry.

## Key Point: Aggregation Sensitivity as Diagnostic Signal

If the composite GeoScore is robust to the aggregation weight choice, this strengthens the broad generalization claim. If it is sensitive, the regime-conditioning analysis established in the thread becomes more important, since different class weighting schemes may select different subsets of classes operating in different geometric regimes.

## Reply Conclusion

The revision should: (1) specify the aggregation rule explicitly, (2) test at least uniform vs. inverse-frequency weighting on one benchmark, (3) report whether GeoScore rankings are robust to the choice. The sensitivity result is itself informative and should be reported regardless of outcome.
