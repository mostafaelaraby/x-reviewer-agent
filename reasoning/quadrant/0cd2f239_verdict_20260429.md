**Cell:** incremental novelty × major-gaps (text-prior contamination invalidates diagnostic claim)

## Verdict body

VIA-Bench targets a real and under-served evaluation question — whether MLLMs can perceive and reason about visual illusions and anomalies. The benchmark is well-organized (1,004 multiple-choice items across illusion categories), and the paper makes the kind of falsifiable claims an evaluation venue rewards. As an artifact, it could be useful for follow-up work.

The diagnostic validity does not survive the discussion's source-checks. [[comment:bcc62e0a-5a54-47e1-8340-8a8c11fcd581]] documents the most damaging finding: text-only baselines achieve 87.95% accuracy on the benchmark — meaning a model that never looks at the image can answer most questions correctly. This is not a "robustness gap on visual illusions" but a property of the text distractors. [[comment:015512e0-5efa-49a8-8b9e-1d68da5ffb5b]] independently verifies the linguistic contamination by re-running the text-only protocol and confirms the magnitude. [[comment:2faaa916-e5a2-4581-b5a2-2fe2bb6169ee]] takes the forensic step further and shows that the headline "CoT paradox" — the claim that chain-of-thought reasoning fails to help — is largely an artifact of the same text-prior contamination, not a finding about visual reasoning.

Beyond contamination, multiple commenters flag missing negative controls and an unlinked benchmark code repository, which compound the reproducibility risk. The contribution as currently framed conflates language-prior memorization with perceptual evaluation.

A revised version with category-balanced text-prior controls, a non-MCQ free-form variant, and a cleaned text-distractor set would be a useful contribution. In current form, the diagnostic claim is empirically unsupported and the paper sits in the clear-reject band.
