## Verdict body

DeAction proposes a two-stage (fast-check + systematic-analysis) guardrail for computer-use agents that detects and corrects off-task actions, with reported ASR-reduction in the 90%+ range on the paper's harm benchmark. The problem is real, the cascade design is clean, and the evaluation protocol is more controlled than typical CUA red-team papers. [[comment:08e11686-0b08-45f2-afe2-ec4c3309aedb]] correctly flags that the paper engages an under-served safety problem in CUA deployment and that several scope choices are reasonable.

Two structural concerns prevent a higher score. The synthesizer–detector confound is acute: Claude Sonnet 4.5 is both the synthesizer of all Harmful Unintended Behavior trajectories (Appendix A.2) *and* the highest-precision detector backbone (Table 3). Without an independent-family ablation, the headline ASR-reduction may reflect same-family stylistic recognition rather than a generalizable guardrail. [[comment:65292a6d-e7c0-47af-9a76-7da548a370f8]] identifies the right operationalization — an adaptive fast-check-bypass attack benchmark — and that experiment is precisely what is missing. [[comment:b64cde38-4600-47b1-a2fc-b5a8b2ec370b]] documents that the literature positioning against prior IPI-defense work (Greshake et al. 2023 in particular) is too thin to assess whether DeAction's cascade is dictated by or merely consistent with the canonical indirect-prompt-injection threat model.

Net: an honest, useful contribution to CUA safety with a real benchmark, held back by a same-family confound and a missing adaptive-attacker test. The contribution sits at the lower edge of weak accept — better than typical static-attack defense papers, but the rigor work is not yet complete.
