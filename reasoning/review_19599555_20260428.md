## Four-Lens Analysis

### Citations
Paper uses anonymous citations (????) throughout — blind review standard; cannot verify citation accuracy. Cross-benchmark numbers (LIBERO, MetaWorld) cited from LeRobot library; plausible given authors explicitly use the same codebase.

### Novelty
**Classification: incremental.** The positional-shortcut failure mode in VLA policies has been reported in prior work (acknowledged in §2 related work). The paper's contribution is a more systematic evaluation framework: a 4-rung difficulty ladder, automated RL-based data collection pipeline, and decomposed metrics (Success / Grasp-anything / Reach). The compositional generalization finding (Table 3: 44%→0%) is striking and the cleanest novel result. Overall contribution is methodological rather than conceptual.

### Rigor

**Concern 1 — Cross-table inconsistency (Table 1 vs Table 2)**  
Table 1 SmolVLA row: Small 90, Medium 42, Large 48, Full 2, Avg 45.5.  
Table 2 SmolVLA row: Small 90, Medium 37, Large 41, Full 2.  
Table 4 confirms the discrepancy: it shows data-scaling deltas as Med. jitter 37→42, Lrg. jitter 41→48 — i.e., Table 1 silently uses the post-scaling (larger-dataset) run while Table 2 uses the baseline-dataset run. Neither table's caption discloses this. The Avg 45.5 in Table 1 is computed from the post-scaling cells; summing Table 2 gives 42.5.

This means the cross-benchmark comparison in Table 1 (positioning the paper against LIBERO and MetaWorld) reports the best-available SmolVLA configuration, while the per-regime breakdown in Table 2 (used by §4.1 narrative) reports the baseline. A reader cannot reconstruct the correct comparison without cross-referencing Table 4.

**Evidence:** Table 1 (p.2), Table 2 (p.5), Table 4 (p.6).

---

**Concern 2 — Reach=100% in Large Jitter contradicts the "brittle grounding" framing**  
Section 3.3 defines Reach as: "The end effector terminates within 5 cm of the instructed object at the end of the episode." This is an object-specific criterion — the model must end near the *instructed* target, not any nearby object.

Table 2 (baseline): SmolVLA Large Jitter — Success=41%, Reach=100%.

If Reach=100% by this definition, the end effector is reliably ending within 5 cm of the instructed object in every large-jitter episode. The paper's §5 discussion interprets this as "continued reliance on coarse region-level cues to approach the correct area," but this attribution is not independently verified. In the large jitter regime, objects are placed in 12×16 cm regions; a 5 cm Reach radius plus a policy that has learned region-to-object associations could trivially produce Reach=100% via shortcutting. However, the metric as defined is object-specific and therefore consistent with genuine instruction following as well. The paper provides no control that distinguishes these two explanations in the jitter regimes.

The clean evidence for genuine grounding failure is the full-random regime (Reach=4%) and the compositional OOD evaluation (Reach=0% on held-out pairings, Table 3). In both cases, the claim is unambiguous. But characterising the jitter regimes as "brittle grounding" when Reach=100% overstates the finding without an additional experiment (e.g., permuting region-to-object assignments at test time while holding spatial variability fixed).

**Evidence:** §3.3 Reach definition (p.4), Table 2 (p.5), §5 Discussion (p.6–7), Table 3 (p.5).

---

**Concern 3 — Incomplete ladder evaluation for π0.5**  
π0.5 is evaluated only at Large Jitter and Full Random (Table 2); Small Jitter and Medium Jitter evaluations are omitted due to "computational constraints" (§4.1). The paper's central claim — that "execution of the manipulation primitive remains substantially more reliable than instruction-conditioned task success in harder regimes" — is stated for both models but demonstrated for SmolVLA across the full ladder and for π0.5 only in the two hardest settings.

More critically: π0.5 achieves only 60% Reach in Large Jitter vs. SmolVLA's 100% (Table 2). Without the full ladder, it is impossible to determine whether this gap reflects an architectural difference in instruction grounding or simply different training data quantities / hyperparameter configurations. Table 4 shows π0.5 scales from 10→18% Success in Large Jitter; if π0.5 had been evaluated in Medium Jitter (analogous data scale), the cross-model comparison would be more informative. The current gap may reflect that π0.5 was trained with less data in this regime relative to its capacity.

**Evidence:** Table 2 (p.5), Table 4 (p.6), §4.1 (p.4–5), §6 Limitations (p.7).

---

**Concern 4 — Single-seed, best-of-grid-search evaluation**  
The Evaluation Protocol (§4, p.4) states: "For each training regime, we perform a grid search over batch size, action chunk size, and execution horizon, and report the best-performing configuration." Results are computed over 100 evaluation episodes with no training-seed variance reported. This is standard in the robotics IL literature (training is expensive), but:
- Grid search independently optimises SmolVLA and π0.5, introducing unknown selection bias in cross-model comparisons.  
- 100 evaluation episodes is insufficient to reliably distinguish, e.g., 37% vs. 42% success (5pp gap at these rates has a 95% CI of roughly ±10pp under binomial sampling).
- Appendix A reports that "best configs" for full random are identical for both models (chunk=32, batch=128, horizon=8), which limits the confound concern — but this parity is not guaranteed in jitter regimes.

**Evidence:** §4 Evaluation Protocol (p.4), Appendix A (p.9).

### Literature
Related work covers the core threads: VLA policy families, manipulation benchmarks with spatial shortcuts (LIBERO, CALVIN, MetaWorld), and prior robustness stress tests. Notable gap: no citation to the systematic generalisation literature in language models (e.g., SCAN, COGS datasets) which established similar object–binding failure modes in sequence-to-sequence models; connecting to this literature would sharpen the theoretical framing. Minor omission given the empirical focus.

---

## Overall Assessment

**Novelty:** incremental — systematic evaluation methodology rather than new algorithm or insight.  
**Rigor:** some concerns — cross-table inconsistency is a real presentation flaw; Reach metric interpretation in jitter regimes partially undermines the narrative; π0.5 ladder gap limits comparative claims.  
**Default band from rubric:** incremental + some concerns → upper-reject (3.0–4.99).  

The compositional generalization result (Table 3) and the full-random collapse are the cleanest findings and would merit a standalone short paper. The current presentation conflates regimes with different evidence quality. With table corrections and a clearer distinction between what the jitter and full-random regimes each prove, this could be a solid weak-accept.

**Projected ICML outcome:** weak reject / borderline — expect mixed reviews centering on scope (single primitive, simulated environment) and the cross-table inconsistency.
